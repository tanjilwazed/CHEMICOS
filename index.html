A Green Chemistry Perspective

Abstract

This paper discusses the development of biodegradable polymers as eco-friendly alternatives to traditional plastics. Derived from sustainable feedstocks like starch, cellulose, and algae, these bioplastics offer a solution to the plastic pollution crisis. The paper investigates their mechanical properties, degradation rates, and environmental impacts, supported by data, graphs, and visual illustrations to evaluate their commercial viability.

Introduction

Traditional plastic, though revolutionary, has turned into a major environmental concern. Over 380 million tons of plastic are produced annually, of which only 9% is recycled. This research aims to explore biodegradable alternatives using a green chemistry approach.

Sustainable Feedstocks

Starch: Derived from corn, potatoes, and wheat. Easily processed, cost-effective, biodegradable.
Cellulose: Abundant plant polymer; good barrier properties.
Algae: Fast-growing, low land use, high yield.

Mechanical Properties Comparison

Polymer Tensile Strength (MPa) Elongation (%) Barrier to Moisture
Conventional PE 30-50 300-600 Low
PLA (from starch) 40-60 10-50 Medium
Algae-based polymer 15-30 5-15 High

Degradation Rate

Traditional plastic takes 450–1000 years to degrade. Bioplastics like PLA degrade within 3–6 months under composting conditions. Algae-based plastics show faster degradation in soil and marine conditions.

Environmental Impact

  • Reduced greenhouse gas emissions
  • Biodegradation minimizes long-term pollution
  • No toxic residue unlike PVC or PS

Conclusion

Bioplastics derived from renewable feedstocks present a promising path forward. The shift toward sustainable packaging is essential for meeting SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption and Production) and SDG 13 (Climate Action).
